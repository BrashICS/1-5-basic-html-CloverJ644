Some information on Sea slugs

What is a Sea slug?

The name sea slug refers to some marine invertebrates with a resemblence (on some level) to terrestrial slugs.
Most sea slugs are gastropods, sea snails that— over evolutionary time— have entirely or at least seemingly lost their shells. I say "seemingly" because some sea slugs have significantly reduced shells, and others have internal shells. For example, nudibranchs and some other marine gastropods without visible shells. (Note: despite looking like and often being referred to as sea slugs, sea cucumbers are not gastropods)

Physiological Facts

Sea slugs come in an extremely wide variety of sizes, shapes, and colors. Most of them are partially translucent! The often bright colors of reef-dwelling sea slugs can be a good indicator of their toxic stinging cells (called nematocysts) or unpleasant taste. Like all gastropods, sea slugs have small, razor sharp teeth called radulas.
Most sea slugs have a pair of rhinopores— a type of chemosensory organ— on their head, each having a small eye on its base, and most have feathery structures called cerata on their backs, which act like gills. All genuine sea slugs have a selected prey animal that they rely on for food, which can include other members of their own species. Despite what you may think, sea slugs do, in fact, have brains! For example, aplysia californica (the California sea hare) has a brain of about 20,000 nerve cells!

Some sea slugs I like

  • Nembrotha aurea, the "Golden Nembrotha"
  • Doriprismatica atromarginata, the "Black-margined Nudibranch"
  • Chelidonura varians, which has many names, including "Blue Doris", "Blue Velvet Headshield Slug", "Blue-Lined Sandflat Slug", and "Hammerhead Slug"
  • And my personal favorite: Jorunna parva, also known as the "Sea Bunny"! And extremely poisonous sea slug with an absolutely adorable appearance!
